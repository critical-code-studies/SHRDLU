The demonstration dialogue

This is the conversation Terry Winograd published as the demonstration of SHRDLU, reproduced verbatim from his own transcript. The person types in lower case; SHRDLU answers in capitals; the parenthetical stage directions, (does it), are Winograd's own. The original typographical quirks are preserved, including the misspelling "ANWHERE" and the irregular spacing, because they are evidence of the transcript's status as a hand-prepared artefact rather than a live trace, which is itself part of what a critical reading of the demo must attend to.

reading the conversation
Commentary

What the dialogue is doing

Read as a text, the conversation is a carefully ordered argument for machine understanding. Each exchange exhibits a distinct capacity, and the order is not accidental.

Reference and ambiguity

"Grasp the pyramid" is met not with an action but with a question: I DON'T UNDERSTAND WHICH PYRAMID YOU MEAN. SHRDLU knows there is more than one pyramid and that the definite article presupposes a unique referent. A few lines later it resolves "it" by spelling out the description it has inferred, "BY 'IT', I ASSUME YOU MEAN…". This is anaphora and presupposition handled in context, the very phenomena that the later Winograd Schema Challenge would make a benchmark.

A model of the world, queried

"What does the box contain?", "what is the pyramid supported by?", "how many blocks are not in the box?" treat the blocks world as a small database of spatial relations. SHRDLU is not pattern-matching on the words; it is consulting a model and computing. "CAN THE TABLE PICK UP BLOCKS? NO." and "CAN A PYRAMID SUPPORT A PYRAMID? I DON'T KNOW." show the difference between what the world rules out and what the program simply has no axiom for. The honest "I DON'T KNOW" is doing real work for the demo's credibility.

The goal stack speaks

The chain of "why?" questions is the most quoted passage, and the most revealing. "TO GET RID OF IT", "TO CLEAN OFF THE RED CUBE", up to "BECAUSE YOU ASKED ME TO", is SHRDLU reading back its own Micro-Planner goal stack. The machine can explain itself because its reasoning was an explicit structure of goals and sub-goals. This legibility is precisely what today's neural systems lack, and it is worth holding onto as a design value even as we read the demo sceptically.

Learning, and refusing

Near the end the person defines a new word, "a 'steeple' is a stack which contains two green cubes and a pyramid", and SHRDLU answers "I UNDERSTAND" and can then build one. It also refuses gracefully: asked why it dropped the superblock, it corrects the false presupposition, "I CAN'T EXPLAIN A NON-EXISTENT EVENT." These are the high points of the demonstration, and the reason it persuaded.

The seam in the artefact

The misspelling "ANWHERE" and the irregular spacing are small but telling. This is a prepared transcript, the dialogue Winograd himself later described as "very carefully worked through, line by line." The conversation is real in that SHRDLU could produce these responses, and staged in that the path through them was chosen in advance. Holding both facts at once is the task of a critical reading: the dialogue is neither a fraud nor a free conversation, but a demonstration, an artefact built to make an argument.
